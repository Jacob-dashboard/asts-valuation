Reading this honestly

What this model underwrites

One steady-state year of AST SpaceMobile's MNO direct-to-device business at whatever scale you set — not a DCF, not a path. The bet being priced: AST becomes the space-based cellular layer that mobile network operators resell to their own subscribers. Revenue is deliberately narrow — MNO D2D only: no government/FirstNet, no IoT, no gateway or spectrum side-deals.

revenue = subscribers(B) × ARPU($/mo) × 12 × revenue_share%

Every subscriber pays a few dollars a month, AST keeps its share, and the P&L waterfall (gross margin → opex → D&A → interest → tax) turns that into three different "earnings" a market could price.

What "one steady-state year" means

The model freezes a single representative year in the future — the constellation built to your satellite setting, subscribers fully ramped, replacement launches running at cruise — and values that snapshot with market multiples. It deliberately skips the path to get there: no discounting of the in-between years, no execution risk, no dilution along the way.

That isn't an oversight — it's what the multiples are for. A 28× P/E on a mature year already embeds a market's judgment about growth and risk; layering a DCF on top would double-count. The division of labor here: this model prices the destination; the Financing tab models the road — year-by-year launches, subscriber ramp, and whether cash on hand survives the trip.

The one discipline it imposes: when you compare the implied price to today's $61.95, remember the implied price belongs to that future steady-state year. The gap between the two is exactly the growth, time, and execution risk you're being paid to underwrite.

AST's own margin guidance — vs what this model assumes

On the Q4 2025 earnings call, management said flow-through margins and operating leverage "could contribute to an EBITDA margin in the 90% area or higher" at scale, with high service gross margins already observed. The logic: a satellite network's costs are mostly fixed — once the constellation exists, each incremental subscriber's revenue flows through at very high margin.

This model deliberately underwrites well below that: at base settings, 70% gross margin minus 13% opex = a 57% EBITDA margin — roughly two-thirds of what the company itself guides to. Even the slider ceiling (70% GM, 5% opex → 65%) can't reach the guided 90%. That gap is margin of safety, not a forecast disagreement: if management is right about 90%+, the base case's $34.2B EBITDA on $60B revenue would be closer to $54B, and every lens re-rates upward. You're pricing the business as a decent telecom, while the company pitches it as a software-margin toll road.

Adoption & the growth premium — without double counting

Subscribers don't appear on day one — they adopt over years. The obvious fix, juicing the multiples for "growth," would double count: a 28× P/E already embeds a market's growth expectation for a mature business. So the model keeps growth out of the multiples and puts it where it belongs — in time:

value_today = steady-state blend price ÷ (1 + discount)^years-to-target

An S-curve (slow start, steep middle, saturating top — how every network technology has actually adopted) says when the subscriber target arrives; the discount rate charges you for waiting and for execution risk along the way. At your current settings: .

Why the early-stage numbers stop being absurd. Frozen at 45 satellites, 100M subs and $2.50 ARPU, the model prints — technically true for that year, useless for underwriting, because nobody owning the stock at 45 satellites is pricing that year forever. With adoption underwriting, that same moment is priced as the path to your subscriber target: today. The frozen number stays visible (it's the honest snapshot); the PV number is the one to compare against the market.

The stage table now carries this too: each stage has an ETA (editable assumption of when it arrives) and a PV today column — its then-price discounted back. Note how the Mature stage, 9 years out, is worth less today than the Worldwide stage at year 6 despite the bigger fleet economics: time value eats the difference. That's the discipline the discount rate buys.

Dilution from the converts — what full conversion costs you

ASTS has four convertible series outstanding. If every dollar converted to stock, share count rises by only ~M shares — about +7% on 388M — because each raise priced conversion at a 20% premium and wrapped it in a capped call:

The Dilution group on the Console has two sliders. "Converts settled in shares" walks between the two worlds: 0% = everything cash-settled or repaid (the company has repurchased converts before — $296.5M in Feb 2026 — and holds the option to settle the 2034 notes in cash); 100% = all four series become stock. At full conversion the diluted count is M and the base blend price moves by roughly −7%. The capped calls (up to $120.12–$149.20) further offset the economic dilution between conversion and cap prices — the slider models gross share settlement, so it's the conservative end. The readout under each slider shows the resulting share count as you drag.

"Future dilution beyond converts" is the second slider: extra shares from raises that haven't happened yet — stock comp, ATM offerings, equity funding for Block 3, whatever comes. It scales the 388M base directly (10% = +38.8M shares). History says assume something: share count grew ~18% in the year to mid-2026. Default is 0% so the base case stays anchored to known securities — dial it up to underwrite management's appetite.

One caveat: if the notes convert it's because the stock is far above the conversion prices — i.e., dilution happens only in the scenarios where the thesis is working. Debt that self-extinguishes into a winning position is the cheapest financing there is.

The three lenses — and why they disagree

1 · EV / Gross Profit at 11×. Anchors on gross profit, before any operating or capital cost exists. This is how pre-scale infrastructure gets pitched — "look at the unit economics." It is the most generous lens by construction, and it is blind to everything that makes a satellite business hard.

eq_gross = gross_profit × 11× − net_debt

2 · EV / EBITDA at 16×. Anchors on EBITDA — it absorbs the cost of running the business but sits above the D&A line, so it never sees that the asset generating the revenue burns up in the atmosphere every 5–7 years. For a company whose fleet must be rebuilt continuously, EBITDA structurally overstates cash generation. 16× is a premium-growth-infrastructure multiple (towers/data centers trade high-teens on EBITDA at scale).

eq_ebitda = ebitda × 16× − net_debt

3 · P/E at 28×. Anchors on net income — the only line that has already paid for depreciation (i.e., satellite replenishment), interest, and tax. It is the only honest lens for a business with a consumable fleet, which is why it produces the lowest value. 28× is a quality-growth-compounder multiple: rich versus telecom (~10–15×), justified only while growth stays high.

eq_pe = net_income × 28× (already equity — no net-debt bridge)

The blend weights 50 / 35 / 15 (net / EBITDA / gross) — deliberately majority-weighted to the P/E lens because it is the only one that doesn't pretend the capex isn't there. The gross-profit lens gets a token 15%: it represents how the market actually prices the story in euphoric stretches, so it earns a seat, but a small one.

blend = 0.50·eq_pe + 0.35·eq_ebitda + 0.15·eq_gross → → /sh on M shares

Stage-by-stage underwriting

Each stage pins fleet size to AST's own coverage guidance, sizes subscribers to what that fleet can physically serve, and assigns the multiple a market typically pays at that point in a growth story — hypergrowth premium early, telecom compression at maturity.

Sanity checks — why they exist

  • Revenue per satellite — divides total revenue by the fleet. Each satellite is a shared cell tower with finite beam capacity; if the model demands more than ~$250M/sat/yr, the physics, not the spreadsheet, is the constraint.
  • EV / Revenue vs telecom 2–4× — mature carriers trade at 2–4× revenue. Anything above that is a growth premium you are consciously underwriting; the model shows you exactly how much.
  • Fleet capacity — subscribers can't exceed satellites × capacity-per-satellite. Demand without coverage is not revenue. AST publishes no direct subs-per-satellite figure; what it does publish: 10 GHz of processing bandwidth per Block 2 BlueBird (10× Block 1), 40 MHz beams at up to 120 Mbps, and ~60 MNO agreements covering 3B+ subscribers. Dividing their own addressable base by their own constellation: 3B ÷ 248 ≈ 12M subscribers per satellite addressable — the model's 8M/sat default sits ~35% below that, which is the right side to err on since "addressable" assumes every partner subscriber signs up.
